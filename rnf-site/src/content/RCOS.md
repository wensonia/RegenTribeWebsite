# RCOS — Regenerative Community Operating System

*A modular operating system for intentional communities*

**Source:** [blueprint.ecohubs.community](https://blueprint.ecohubs.community) by [EcoHubs](https://ecohubs.community)  
**License:** Open-source  
**Version:** RCOS v1.0

---

## What Is RCOS?

RCOS is a **modular governance framework** — an operating system for intentional communities. It makes implicit social structures *explicit* so communities can design, stress-test, and evolve their operations before conflicts arise.

The core principle: **Explicit beats Implicit.**

RCOS is designed for communities of **5–150 people** and structured as **7 independent layers** (Layer 0 through Layer 6). Each layer has a stable boundary — communities adapt details via modules without altering the invariants that hold the community together.

---

## The 7 Layers at a Glance

| Layer | Name | Core Question |
|-------|------|--------------|
| **Layer 0** | Identity & Scope | Why does this community exist, and what does it govern? |
| **Layer 1** | Membership | How do people join, participate, and leave? |
| **Layer 2** | Governance | Who decides what, and how? |
| **Layer 3** | Economy & Resources | How do we manage value flows, contributions, and commons? |
| **Layer 4** | Conflict, Repair & Accountability | How do we handle disputes and hold each other accountable? |
| **Layer 5** | Operations & Coordination | How do we organize daily work without burnout? |
| **Layer 6** | Evolution | How do we adapt and improve over time? |

## Layer 0 — Identity & Scope

**Core question:** Why does this community exist, and what does it govern?

Layer 0 establishes the foundational *why* and *what* of the community. It defines:

- **Purpose statement** — The reason the community exists
- **Governed elements** — What the community explicitly governs (assets, domains, activities)
- **Invariants** — Non-negotiable rules that cannot be changed (e.g., core values, safety standards)
- **Out-of-scope** — What the community explicitly does *not* govern (prevents hidden ideology or scope creep)

### Why It Matters

Most community conflict comes from unstated assumptions about what the community is "about." Layer 0 makes these explicit from day one. It draws a clear boundary: inside the scope = governed; outside = out-of-bounds.

### Key Sub-Questions

- What is our purpose?
- What assets, domains, and activities do we explicitly govern?
- What would break the community's identity? (those are your invariants)
- What are we *not* doing?

### RCOS Stress-Tests for Layer 0

- Does the purpose statement actually guide decisions?
- Are invariants genuinely non-negotiable, or just preferences?
- Is anything missing from the scope that should be included?

## Layer 1 — Membership

**Core question:** How do people join, participate, and leave?

Layer 1 defines the complete membership lifecycle:

- **Entry pathways** — How newcomers join (application, trial period, sponsorship, etc.)
- **Participation rights** — What members can do, access, and decide
- **Status changes** — How members move between roles or levels
- **Exit paths** — How and why members leave (voluntary, involuntary, graduated)
- **Obligations symmetry** — Rights and duties are balanced — no special classes

### Key Sub-Questions

- What are the stages of membership?
- What rights does each status grant?
- What happens if someone wants to leave?
- How do we handle involuntary exits?

### RCOS Stress-Tests for Layer 1

- Are there elite classes with special rights?
- Can members exit without penalty or stigma?
- Is the trial period long enough to surface real alignment?

---

## Layer 2 — Governance

**Core question:** Who decides what, and how?

Layer 2 makes decision-making explicit:

- **Decision rights** — Who has authority over what domains
- **Authority constraints** — What no one can decide alone
- **Delegation patterns** — How authority flows to roles and circles
- **Protocols** — Specific procedures for making different types of decisions
- **Constitutional vs. operational separation** — Day-to-day operations vs. fundamental rule changes (kept separate for stability)

### Key Sub-Questions

- What types of decisions require what process (majority, consensus, single person)?
- How are roles assigned and rotated?
- What are the constraints on founders and leaders?
- How do we distinguish operational decisions from constitutional ones?

### RCOS Stress-Tests for Layer 2

- Can founders become authoritarian without accountability?
- Are there review mechanisms for concentrated power?
- Do decisions actually get made efficiently, or does everything stall?

---

## Layer 3 — Economy & Resources

**Core question:** How do we manage value flows, contributions, and commons?

Layer 3 governs the economic layer:

- **Value flows** — How money and resources move through the community
- **Commons vs. private resources** — What is shared vs. individually owned
- **Contributions** — How labor, care work, and intangible inputs are tracked and valued
- **Treasuries** — How community funds are managed and accessed
- **Anti-privatization** — Preventing invisible enclosure of common wealth

### Key Sub-Questions

- What is commons, what is private?
- How do we value and track contributions fairly (including care work)?
- Who controls treasury access?
- How do we prevent resource privatization?

### RCOS Stress-Tests for Layer 3

- Is care work (cooking, cleaning, emotional labor) actually valued?
- Can a small group capture common resources for private gain?
- Are treasury decisions transparent to all members?

---

## Layer 4 — Conflict, Repair & Accountability

**Core question:** How do we handle disputes and hold each other accountable?

Layer 4 provides a structured "resolution ladder" for handling conflict:

- **Conflict pathways** — Step-by-step process for raising and resolving disputes
- **Escalation steps** — What happens when initial resolution fails
- **Safety provisions** — How to protect members during conflicts
- **Repair mechanisms** — How to rebuild trust after a breach
- **Due process** — Fair hearing for all parties before consequences

### Key Sub-Questions

- What is the first step when a conflict arises?
- Who can escalate, and to whom?
- How do we protect the person raising a concern from retaliation?
- How do we repair relationships after a conflict?

### RCOS Stress-Tests for Layer 4

- Do members know how to use the conflict process, or does conflict go underground?
- Can someone be excluded informally without due process?
- Is there a "resolution ladder" or does everything get escalated to the same level?

---

## Layer 5 — Operations & Coordination

**Core question:** How do we organize daily work without burnout?

Layer 5 enables the community to function day-to-day:

- **Role clarity** — Every role has a clear description, scope, and deliverables
- **Meeting structures** — Regular meetings with clear purposes and formats
- **Documentation** — Decisions, agreements, and processes are recorded
- **Workload limits** — Preventing heroics and burnout through sustainable pace
- **Coordination mechanisms** — How work gets organized across teams and circles

### Key Sub-Questions

- Is every role description current and clear?
- Are meetings serving their stated purpose, or are they performative?
- Is there a single place to find how things work?
- How do we prevent reliance on "hero" members who do everything?

### RCOS Stress-Tests for Layer 5

- Does work actually get distributed, or does it accumulate on a few?
- Are meetings producing outcomes, or just discussion?
- Can someone new figure out how to contribute within 24 hours?

---

## Layer 6 — Evolution

**Core question:** How do we adapt and improve over time?

Layer 6 enables controlled, learning-based change:

- **Versioning** — How the community's own rules and agreements can change
- **Learning frameworks** — Regular retrospectives, retrospectives with teeth
- **Stress-testing** — Ongoing testing of each layer against real conditions
- **Change control** — Preventing whimsical or self-serving alterations to core structures

### Key Sub-Questions

- How can the community's own rules be changed? By whom, with what process?
- Are we learning from failures, or just filing them away?
- How do we distinguish genuine improvement from captured change?
- How do we prevent the community from drifting away from its purpose?

### RCOS Stress-Tests for Layer 6

- Have we actually changed anything based on past failures?
- Can a well-resourced actor capture the evolution process?
- Is change too easy (chaos) or too hard (stagnation)?

## How RCOS Relates to Other Frameworks

| Framework | Role | Relationship |
|-----------|------|-------------|
| **Community Alchemy** | Sequential step-by-step journey (vision → land → funding → build → live) | RCOS fills the *governance and operations* gaps within each Alchemy step |
| **RN 5-Spiral Model** | Timeline and milestones for community development | RCOS Layer 2 (Governance) maps to Alchemy Step 3; RCOS Layer 5 (Operations) maps to Spiral 4 |
| **CLIPS** | Inner work + personal/community health checks | CLIPS fills the *individual readiness* layer that RCOS doesn't address |

### Failure Factor Comparison

| Failure Factor | RN Framework | Community Alchemy | RCOS |
|--------------|--------------|-------------------|------|
| Unclear vision | ✓ Spiral 1 | ✓ Step 1 | — |
| Financial failure | ✓ Financial modeling | ✓ Step 4 | ✓ Layer 3 |
| Governance collapse | ✓ Human Systems pillar | ✓ Step 3 | ✓ Layer 2, 4 |
| Member conflict | ✓ Human Systems pillar | ✓ Step 3 | ✓ Layer 4 |
| Operational burnout | ✓ Spiral 4 | ✓ Step 10 | ✓ Layer 5 |

## How to Apply RCOS

### For New Communities

1. Start with **Layer 0** (Identity & Scope) — before anything else
2. Build **Layer 1** (Membership) and **Layer 2** (Governance) before people join
3. Add Layer 3, 4, 5 in parallel as the community grows
4. Layer 6 is ongoing from day one

### For Existing Communities

1. **Audit first** — Run the RCOS stress-tests as diagnostics
2. Ask: "What's frustrated members lately?" — that'll point to the broken layer
3. Don't impose top-down — surface pain points and address them with the affected members
4. Layer 4 (Conflict) is often the most urgent to formalize in existing communities

### Practical Starting Sequence

| Priority | Layer | Why |
|----------|-------|-----|
| 🔴 Urgent | Layer 4 | Conflict processes prevent community death |
| 🟡 Important | Layer 2 | Unclear governance causes most friction |
| 🟡 Important | Layer 3 | Economic fairness prevents resentment |
| 🟢 Foundation | Layer 0 | Purpose drift destroys alignment |
| 🟢 Foundation | Layer 1 | Unclear membership creates boundary conflicts |
| 🔵 Ongoing | Layer 5 + 6 | Operational clarity and learning loops |

## Key Vocabulary

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **Layer** | One of the 7 independent structural areas of RCOS |
| **Module** | Adaptable details within a layer — changeable without altering invariants |
| **Invariant** | Non-negotiable rule that cannot be changed (layer-specific) |
| **Explicit vs. Implicit** | RCOS principle — written, agreed, visible beats unwritten, assumed |
| **Role** | Explicit bundle of responsibilities, authority, and accountability — attached to a position, not a person |
| **Scope** | Explicit declaration of what the community governs vs. out-of-scope |
| **Stewardship** | Responsibility for care and maintenance of a resource within defined boundaries |
| **Stress-test** | A diagnostic exercise that probes whether a layer will hold under pressure |
| **Resolution Ladder** | Step-by-step escalation path for resolving conflicts |
