# CLIPS — Community Learning Incubator Programme for Sustainability

*A framework for community-led project development*

**Source:** GEN-Europe (Global Ecovillage Network), 2017  
**License:** Creative Commons CC BY-NC-ND  
**Origin:** Developed by ecovillage practitioners from 9 European countries, funded by Erasmus+

---

## What Is CLIPS?

CLIPS is a **solution-oriented incubation programme** that guides community-led projects through their initial steps — and revitalizes existing initiatives that struggle with problems or need new momentum.

The purpose of CLIPS is to suggest and explain a **framework for approaching community development**, pointing out specific tools that existing community-led projects have found useful.

CLIPS is based on learnings and insights from **established ecovillage projects**, distilled into concepts and methods applicable to any group that recognizes the value of community.

### What is "community" in CLIPS?

A group of people connected by worldview, vision and goals, sharing at least some parts of their non-professional lives. This includes ecovillages, co-housings, NGOs, civil initiatives, business collectives, community schools, ethical banks, health circles, cooperatives, community-supported agriculture projects, etc.

### What is an ecovillage?

An ecovillage is a type of community that uses **local participatory processes** to integrate ecological, economic, social, and cultural dimensions of sustainability in order to **regenerate** social and natural environments.

### What is incubation?

A **facilitated process** supporting a group to identify tensions, solutions, resources and methods, applying appropriate measures at appropriate times. Like midwives — facilitators assist groups during the delivery of their project and check in as it matures.

> ⚠️ Incubation requires attention and care, but **should never be authoritarian**.

## The CLIPS Model — 5 Layers

CLIPS uses a **concentric circles model** to show the complex dynamics and layers found in community projects:

```
         Practice
          /    \
    Structure  Structure
       |          |
      Intention  Intention
      /              \
    Community ---- Individual
         (dual core)
```

### The 5 Layers

| Layer | Name | Core Question |
|-------|------|--------------|
| **1** | **Individual** | Who are the people, and how do they grow? |
| **2** | **Community** | How do we build relationships and shared culture? |
| **3** | **Intention** | Why do we exist, and where are we going? |
| **4** | **Structure** | How do we organize ourselves? |
| **5** | **Practice** | How do we actually do things? |

### The Dual Core: Individual ↔ Community

These two layers are in the centre because they are **crucial for healthy development** of any project:

- **There is no thriving community without thriving individuals**
- **Functional community is very conducive to individual growth**

The next step is setting **Intentions**. Then comes creation of functioning **Structures**. Finally, the layer of actual **Practice** takes shape.

### Key Principle: Coherence Between Layers

Ideally Individual, Community, Intention, Structure, and Practice are **perfectly interlinked**.

However, in practice:
- **Practice is not always linked well to Intentions**
- **Structures can be missing or inadequate**
- **Intentions can suffer from not being revisited**

> **Coherence between Intention, Structure, and Practice** — and the **balance between Individual and Community** — are under constant challenge. Reasons for conflicts and malfunctions can generally be traced to **not paying enough attention to interconnection between the layers** or to **neglecting one of the layers**.

## Layer 1 — The Individual

**Core question:** Who are the people, and how do they grow?

### Overview

Every community consists of individuals. A community can thrive **only if individuals are honoured and respected**. Individuals tend to thrive in community if they approach life with a **learner's attitude** — acknowledging that all they encounter is part of the learning journey and therefore valuable, even if different than anticipated.

Individual growth and community building work best in tandem when tools for **deep sharing and feedback** are consciously applied.

### Key Points

- Creating new ways of living or solving societal problems can be challenging and require people to change and adapt in unexpected ways
- A mature, skillful and responsible way of dealing with emotions, beliefs, habits and attitudes is an important ingredient in successful community projects
- Cultivating a **learner's attitude** enables personal growth and community building to work in tandem

### Personal Background

Each person brings a unique history, set of beliefs, habits, and patterns of behaviour. Being in community requires members to view their own beliefs, needs, and priorities **in relationship to** the intention, agreements and aims of the group — and in relationship to others.

### Personal Intention and Commitment

The willingness to create or live in community indicates a person has a certain set of **attitudes**:
- A willingness to take initiative and explore new territory
- A wish to live in a different way than before
- A longing to change the world for the better
- A desire to live more in alignment with one's ideals and values

While positive and creative, the step to turn the search for alternatives into actual everyday life also **challenges each individual's habits, beliefs, and patterns of behaviour** — things that often go deeper than expected.

## Layer 2 — The Community

**Core question:** How do we build relationships and shared culture?

### Overview

Community building does not happen by itself — it **must be fostered consciously**. Among basic requirements are common joyful activities and sharing in everyday life. Conflicts and difficulties are opportunities for designing appropriate organisation that supports community spirit.

The form of organisation and the means of conflict resolution should be **complementary**.

### Group Identity and Community Spirit

Building a sense of "we" — shared identity, shared purpose, shared pride in the community.

### Communication Culture

How the group talks, listens, shares, and resolves misunderstandings.

### Rituals and Celebration

Regular practices that mark time, celebrate milestones, and reinforce shared meaning.

### How to Run Meetings

Structured gatherings that produce outcomes, honor everyone's voice, and don't waste time.

### Sharing Joyful Activities, Creativity and Art

Shared fun, creative expression, and play as the glue of community.

### Relationship: Love, Care, Sexuality

The emotional layer — intimacy, care, boundaries, and healthy relating.

### Dealing with Conflict

Conflicts and difficulties are **opportunities** for designing appropriate organisation. When handled well, conflict deepens trust and sharpens structures.

### Summary — The Community Layer

## Layer 3 — Intention

**Core question:** Why do we exist, and where are we going?

### Overview

Shared intention takes people **beyond the assumption** that "we all want to go in the same direction". A clear, distinct intention gives orientation — especially in times when the group loses its track and is unclear which way to go.

Unclear intention can become a **source of conflict, confusion and misunderstanding** — therefore clarity should be pursued by all means.

A sign of clarity is that members find intention statements (vision, mission, aim, and purpose) **meaningful and identify with them**.

### Positioning in Society

Where the community stands in relation to the wider world — its role, its public face, its place in the broader movement.

### Vision, Mission, Goals

- **Vision** — What future we are creating
- **Mission** — Why we exist, what we do and for whom
- **Goals** — Specific, time-bound targets that move us toward the vision

### Meeting Minutes

Recording decisions, rationale, and accountabilities — making intentions visible and traceable.

### Adaptation & Resilience

How the community responds to change, learns from experience, and builds resilience.

### External Relations and Networks

Connections to other communities, networks, organisations, and the wider world.

### Summary — Intention Layer

## Layer 4 — Structure

**Core question:** How do we organize ourselves?

### Overview

Efficient cooperation requires **shared agreements** — for instance on decision-making, organising finances, legal representation, etc. These belong to the layer of Structure, and are, sadly, often **neglected**.

**Unclear structures are among key causes of long-term problems in group projects.**

On the other hand, well-developed structures, when disconnected from other layers, tend to be **repressive and short-term**.

### Governance

The overall framework of how the community is led, who has authority, and how the community holds itself accountable.

### Decision-Making

How choices get made — the processes, forums, and rights that govern collective decisions.

### Access to Information

Transparency — who knows what, how information flows, and who controls it.

### Property and Legal Organisation

Ownership, land tenure, assets, legal structures, and the formal relationship between members and the community's property.

### Constitution

The foundational written agreement that defines the community's purpose, rules, membership, and governance.

### Economic Organisation

How money, resources, labour, and value circulate — contributions, sharing, and economic sustainability.

### Rank and Leadership

How status, recognition, and authority are distributed — and how to prevent rank from distorting community health.

### Project Management, Delegation of Work and Task Assignment

Who does what, how work is coordinated, and how tasks are assigned and tracked.

### Feedback

Mechanisms for giving and receiving input on how things are going — at individual, group, and system levels.

### Summary — Structure Layer

## Layer 5 — Practice

**Core question:** How do we actually do things?

### Overview

The CLIPS guide focuses on the first four layers. The fifth layer, Practice, is covered with a few examples that seek to illustrate the connections between the layers.

Practice is where **intentions and structures meet the real world**. It is also where they most often diverge.

### Example 1: Shared Dinners

How an everyday practice can build community culture, reinforce shared rhythms, and become a backbone of social connection.

### Example 2: Check-ins and Moments of Awareness

Brief structured sharing practices that build emotional intelligence, surface tensions early, and keep the Individual and Community layers alive.

### Example 3: Compost Toilets

A practical systems example showing how a technical decision (waste management) touches Structure (who decides), Intention (environmental values), Community (shared responsibility), and Individual (comfort and habits).

### Example 4: Car Sharing

Resource management practice showing how shared agreements about high-value shared assets require clear Structure, clear Intention, and practiced Community norms.

### Practice Stress-Test

When Practice disconnects from Intention and Structure:
- **Symptoms:** "We have all these agreements but nobody follows them"
- **Symptoms:** "Our stated values say X but we always do Y"
- **Symptoms:** "We keep having the same argument over and over"

## How CLIPS Integrates with RNF and RCOS

| CLIPS Layer | RNF Pillar | RCOS Layer | Best For |
|------------|-----------|-----------|----------|
| **Individual** | Human Systems (inner work, personal readiness) | Layer 0 (Identity & Scope) | Personal readiness, self-awareness |
| **Community** | Human Systems (relationships, culture, safety) | Layer 1 (Membership) | Group dynamics, shared culture |
| **Intention** | All pillars (vision + purpose alignment) | Layer 0 (Purpose) | Vision, mission, strategic direction |
| **Structure** | Human Systems + Economy (governance, legal, economic) | Layers 2, 3, 4 (Governance, Economy, Conflict) | Decision-making, roles, legal, finance |
| **Practice** | All 5 pillars (operational execution) | Layers 5, 6 (Operations, Evolution) | Day-to-day operations, learning loops |

### CLIPS vs. RCOS — Key Distinction

| Dimension | CLIPS | RCOS |
|-----------|-------|------|
| **Origin** | Ecovillage practitioners (GEN-Europe, 2017) | EcoHubs community infrastructure |
| **Structure** | 5 concentric layers (Individual + Community at core) | 7 flat layers (Layer 0–6) |
| **Core insight** | Individual ↔ Community balance is the foundation | Explicit beats implicit — governance must be stated |
| **Primary contribution** | Depth on Individual + Community layers | Operational stress-tests + modular templates |
| **Weakness** | Less detailed on conflict repair and accountability | Less emphasis on personal readiness and group culture |

### CLIPS Failure Factor Mapping

| Failure Factor | CLIPS Addresses Via |
|---------------|---------------------|
| Individual burnout, unmet personal needs | ✓ Layer 1 |
| Group culture collapse | ✓ Layer 2 |
| Purpose/vision drift | ✓ Layer 3 |
| Governance breakdown | ✓ Layer 4 |
| Practice-Intention disconnect | ✓ Layer 5 |

## Facilitator Notes

### How to Facilitate Groups with CLIPS

CLIPS facilitators assist groups during the delivery of their project, and check in on it as it matures. To stand as a CLIPS facilitator requires:
- Substantial community experience
- A wide peer network
- Specialised training (available via CLIPS website)

### The CLIPS Model in Use

The model should be applied **adventurously** — concepts from the guide can be used whenever the facilitator deems appropriate. Incubation requires attention and care, but should **never be authoritarian**.

### Recommendations for Training of Trainers (ToT)

The CLIPS website provides information on:
- Pilot Trainings
- Trainings of Trainers for trainers with different levels of experience
